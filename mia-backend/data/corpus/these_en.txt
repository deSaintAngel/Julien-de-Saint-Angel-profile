# PhD thesis of Julien de Saint Angel — multi-level knowledge base

## [Overview] The thesis subject in one sentence
Julien de Saint Angel's PhD is about a new kind of neural network — "hyperspherical layers" — built from conformal geometric algebra, and their application to anomaly detection. In one sentence: he invented a geometric way of doing AI, where neurons use spheres instead of straight lines, to better spot abnormal data.
Exact title: "Réseaux de neurones à couches hypersphériques ; Application à la détection d'anomalies" (English title: "Hyperspherical neural networks for anomaly detection"). Defended on 4 July 2025 at La Rochelle University (MIA laboratory, Euclide doctoral school).
Keywords: thesis, subject, summary, PhD, neural networks, hyperspheres, hyperspherical, conformal geometric algebra, anomaly detection, one sentence, what is it about.

## [Plain language] The thesis explained simply
Imagine a machine that must tell what is "normal" and flag what is "abnormal": a defective part on a production line, a suspicious bank transaction, an irregular heartbeat. Most classical neural networks draw "straight lines" (flat boundaries) to separate cases. Julien proposed using "bubbles" instead — spheres — that surround the normal data. Anything inside the bubble is normal; anything outside is an anomaly. This idea, formalized with an elegant branch of mathematics (conformal geometric algebra), makes the model more accurate on complex data, more stable, and above all easier to understand: you can literally "see" why it made a decision.

## [Plain language] What is anomaly detection?
Detecting an anomaly means spotting an element that stands out, that does not look like the others. Everyday examples: an unusual charge on your bank card, a machine overheating abnormally, a suspicious cell on a medical image, a defect on a product. The difficulty: we mostly know "normal" examples and very few anomalies (they are rare and varied). Julien's work learns to finely describe what is "normal" so that any deviation becomes detectable, even when the data is complex and does not follow a simple shape.
Keywords: anomaly detection, anomaly, outlier, abnormal, fraud, defect, failure, rare, spot, detect, concrete example, what is it for.

## [Overview] The scientific problem of anomaly detection
Anomaly detection identifies points that deviate from the distribution of "normal" data. Classical methods (Elliptic Envelope, Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, Support Vector Data Description) reach their limits with the non-linearity and geometric complexity of real data: non-Gaussian, multimodal, high-dimensional distributions. The thesis proposes models that directly learn the enveloping geometry of normal data using hyperspheres, gaining robustness and interpretability.
Keywords: anomaly detection, one-class, distribution, non-linear, multimodal, high-dimensional, isolation forest, one-class svm, svdd, state of the art, problem, robustness, interpretability.

## [Plain language] What are "hyperspherical layers"?
In an ordinary neural network, each neuron draws a flat boundary (like a sheet of paper cutting space in two). Julien replaced these flat boundaries with spheres: each "hyperspherical" neuron defines a bubble in space. This is more natural for surrounding a group of data, and a sphere can hug shapes that straight lines miss. You keep all the advantages of deep networks, but with a richer, more readable geometry.
Keywords: hyperspherical layers, hypersphere, sphere, bubble, neuron, boundary, hyperplane, straight line, flat, curved, explain, simple, plain language.

## [Technical] Definition of hyperspherical layers
Hyperspherical layers replace the classical separating hyperplanes of neural networks with learnable hyperspheres (center and radius learned by gradient descent). They come in dense and convolutional (hyperspherical Conv2D) variants. Formally, they rely on Hestenes' conformal model, which embeds R^n into an augmented vector space R^{n+1,1}, where a hypersphere is represented by a single vector. The conformal inner product (x̃ · is̃) measures whether a point belongs to the sphere; normalization constraints apply (s̃² = ρ², e∞ · is̃ = −1). These layers geometrically generalize RBF networks and Clifford networks, and allow non-linear quadratic separations.
Keywords: hyperspherical layer, hyperplane, learnable hypersphere, center, radius, dense, convolutional, Conv2D, conformal model, Hestenes, conformal inner product, normalization, RBF, Clifford, quadratic, technical, definition.

## [Plain language] Conformal geometric algebra, explained simply
It is a mathematical language that lets you manipulate geometric objects — points, lines, planes, spheres — as simple "bricks" combined with unified rules. Its advantage: a sphere and a plane are described the same way, which makes calculations elegant and consistent. Julien uses it to give neurons a spherical geometry while keeping computations clean and interpretable.
Keywords: conformal geometric algebra, CGA, geometry, mathematical language, sphere, plane, point, unified, explain simply, plain language, what is it for.

## [Technical] Conformal geometric algebra (CGA) — formal framework
The formalism rests on the conformal model of geometry (Hestenes): Euclidean space R^n is embedded into R^{n+1,1} by adding two isotropic basis vectors (origin e0 and infinity e∞). In this algebra, hyperplanes and hyperspheres are represented uniformly by vectors; incidence (does a point lie on the sphere?) is read via the inner product. This unified hyperplane/hypersphere representation is the theoretical foundation of the thesis: it allows defining geometrically consistent neural layers and reasoning about decision boundaries.
Keywords: conformal geometric algebra, conformal, Hestenes, R^{n+1,1}, e0, e_infinity, isotropic, vector, incidence, inner product, unified, hyperplane, hypersphere, formalism, theoretical framework.

## [Technical] SVDD and the proposed algorithms (SPH Anomaly, SPH SVDD, Deep SPH SVDD, Deep M-SPH SVDD)
Support Vector Data Description (SVDD, Tax & Duin) seeks the center c and radius ρ of the smallest hypersphere enclosing the data (in a kernel feature space), with slack variables; the parameter ν ∈ ]0,1] controls tolerance (C = 1/(νK)). Normal points stay inside, anomalies fall outside. In deep learning there are Deep soft-boundary SVDD and One-Class Deep SVDD (Ruff et al.). The thesis proposes four original algorithms (exact spelling, no hyphen): (1) SPH Anomaly — one layer of several hyperspheres, output = weighted sum of sigmoids of the conformal inner products (inspired by RBFDD); (2) Deep SPH Anomaly — SPH Anomaly preceded by a pre-trained feature extractor (autoencoder); (3) SPH SVDD — reformulation of SVDD with a single-neuron hyperspherical layer in conformal algebra; (4) Deep SPH SVDD — SPH SVDD preceded by an encoder, where ALL hypersphere parameters (including the center) are learned, unlike Deep SVDD. Deep M-SPH SVDD is the multi-sphere extension of Deep SPH SVDD (J spheres, score = minimum over the spheres); it adds two loss terms: non-intersection of spheres and non-inclusion of a center in another sphere, to handle multimodal data. The two flagship contributions are Deep SPH SVDD and Deep M-SPH SVDD.
Keywords: SVDD, Support Vector Data Description, enclosing hypersphere, center, radius, inside, outside, soft-boundary, one-class, Deep SVDD, SPH Anomaly, Deep SPH Anomaly, SPH SVDD, Deep SPH SVDD, Deep M-SPH SVDD, multi-sphere, multimodal, nu parameter, contribution.

## [Plain language] Deep M-SPH SVDD, explained simply
Instead of a single big bubble to enclose all normal data, Deep M-SPH SVDD uses several small bubbles, each for a "type" of normal behavior. Example: if "normal" covers several different families of cases, a single bubble would be too big and let anomalies slip through; several bubbles fit reality better. Result: finer, more interpretable detection.
Keywords: Deep M-SPH SVDD, multi-sphere, several bubbles, groups, multimodal, explain simply, plain language, precision, interpretable, example.

## [Technical] Initialization method for hyperspherical layers
A key contribution is a dedicated initialization method for hyperspherical layers, guaranteeing numerical stability and convergence. It makes it possible to stack several hyperspherical layers, which a standard initialization (Glorot/Xavier) does not allow. Why Glorot fails: the hyperspherical operator squares the data, turning a normal distribution into a Gamma-type (then generalized Gamma) one — the normality Glorot assumes is destroyed, causing standard-deviation blow-up and loss of symmetry from the first layer. The method relies on asymptotic properties of generalized Gamma distributions (stable under raising to a power) and on covariance computations (detailed in Appendix A, including the convolutional case). Two key ideas, opposite to Glorot: (a) reduce the variance of the input data; (b) initialize the centers with a NON-zero mean (µ ≠ 0). A scale factor δ enforces equal input/output means and variances. Result: one can stack about a hundred hyperspherical layers without divergence.
Keywords: initialization, convergence, stability, stacking, hundred layers, Glorot, Xavier, Gamma distribution, generalized Gamma, square, covariance, variance, non-zero mean, scale factor, asymptotic, method, contribution.

## [Technical] Approximation theorem for hyperspherical neurons
The thesis establishes an approximation theorem: a network with one hidden hyperspherical layer, with a continuous compactly-supported non-zero activation, uniformly approximates any continuous function on every compact set. Important nuance: this is proven for scalar functions of ONE variable (n=1); the author stresses it is therefore not "universal in the broad sense" (it extends to the L^1 case, and an R→R result extends in principle to R^n→R^n' via Pinkus). The proof relies on Laurent Schwartz's theorem on mean-periodic functions (Annals of Mathematics, 1947): approximation by linear combinations of translates of a function — the hyperspherical output centered at c is a translate. Not to be confused with Pinkus's theorem (used for classical dense layers). Where classical layers produce linear boundaries, hyperspherical layers produce quadratic ones; expressiveness is at least equivalent to MLPs. The thesis also proposes a new activation, Gaussxδ (learned δ), effective on 1D test functions.
Keywords: approximation theorem, one variable, scalar, not universal in the broad sense, continuous function, compact, translates, Laurent Schwartz, mean-periodic functions, Pinkus, RBF, quadratic, MLP, Gaussxδ, activation, GRETSI.

## [Technical] The collapse phenomenon and its prevention
"Collapse" is a known pitfall of classical Deep SVDD: if the center and radius are left free, the network converges to the trivial solution (zero weights, constant output, zero radius → sphere reduced to a point), making detection useless; hence Ruff et al. fix the center and radius. The thesis first proves that every "learning + SVDD" method admits this trivial solution. It then shows why Deep SPH SVDD escapes it: its conformal formulation (where a single vector encapsulates both center AND radius) is not strictly convex; near the trivial solution the gradient w.r.t. the network tends to zero BUT the gradient w.r.t. the radius-encapsulating parameter does not vanish (about 2|1−ν|/ν, non-zero since ν<1). This instability makes the center-radius pair oscillate and prevents reaching the degenerate solution — and it cannot be removed by lowering the learning rate. Induced benefit: Deep SPH SVDD can use learnable biases and bounded activations (Tanh, Sigmoid), forbidden in Deep SVDD. Full justification in Appendix C.
Keywords: collapse, trivial solution, Deep SVDD, zero radius, degeneracy, gradient, not strictly convex, nu parameter, bias, bounded activation, Tanh, prevention, appendix C.

## [Overview] Datasets and experimental results
Datasets: in Chapter 1 (layers and initialization), 2D synthetics named "Easy" and "Dif", MNIST (for convolutions), and — to validate the initialization — a 4D synthetic, a Gaussian mixture, a very high-dimensional synthetic, and the Iris dataset (a 10-layer network reaching roughly 96.7% to 98.3% accuracy depending on the number of hyperspheres). In Chapter 3 (anomaly detection), several 2D synthetics (make blob, make moon, make aniso, make varied, point anomaly, etc.), the modified "easy" set, then MNIST and CIFAR-10 (one-vs-all protocol). Metrics: AUC-ROC (primary), AUC-PR (suited to imbalance) and F1-score. Results: Deep SPH SVDD dominates on the 2D synthetics; on MNIST scores are high (often 0.95–0.99); CIFAR-10 is harder; on a CIFAR subset, Deep M-SPH SVDD (0.71) beats the DMSVDD baseline (0.663). In very high dimension the network tends to use a single sphere. Optimizer Adam, implementations in TensorFlow and PyTorch.
Keywords: results, experiments, datasets, Iris, MNIST, CIFAR-10, Easy, Dif, make moon, make blob, synthetic, AUC-ROC, AUC-PR, F1-score, DMSVDD, nu parameter, performance, Adam, benchmarks, validation.

## [Overview] Main contributions of the thesis
1) A unified geometric formalism (conformal algebra) representing hyperplanes and hyperspheres. 2) Dense and convolutional hyperspherical neural layers (implemented in PyTorch and TensorFlow). 3) An initialization method guaranteeing stability and convergence. 4) A universal approximation theorem for these networks. 5) The anomaly-detection models SPH-SVDD, Deep SPH-SVDD and Deep M-SPH SVDD. 6) Experimental validation showing gains in performance, stability and interpretability.
Keywords: contributions, novelty, formalism, layers, initialization, theorem, SPH-SVDD, Deep M-SPH SVDD, PyTorch, TensorFlow, summary of contributions.

## [Plain language] What are these results useful for?
These methods apply wherever you must spot the abnormal: industrial monitoring (detecting a failing machine or part), time series (sensors, signals), high-frequency sports diagnostics, image analysis (including medical), cybersecurity and finance (fraud). The extra strength of Julien's approach is interpretability: you can explain why a case was judged abnormal, which is crucial in sensitive domains.

## [Overview] Why interpretability is at the heart of the thesis
Many AI models are "black boxes": powerful but impossible to explain. By giving neurons an explicit spherical geometry, Julien's approach makes decision boundaries readable: each decision relates to a position with respect to learned spheres. This geometric interpretability is a guiding thread of the thesis, motivated by the need for trust in real-world applications.
Keywords: interpretability, explainability, black box, trustworthy AI, decision boundary, geometry, transparent, understandable, why.
